Supervised Short Text Similarity using Machine Learning

Published: Tue 24 April 2018

In this post we will explore supervised machine learning techniques to identify semantically similar legal text provisions. The objective is same as the last post, to find the transposing NIM provisions for a particular article of the directive. But in this case we will utilize labelled training data. The dataset is annotated by legal professionals. If a NIM provision, P transposes a directive article, A then they are considered to be similar (represented by "YES" label). The dataset has the following format.

We have two labels, "YES" and "NO". "YES" implies a transposition and also that the texts are similar. Whereas, "NO" implies not a transposition (and also that texts are not similar). Therefore, this becomes a binary classification problem. With sufficient training data for both labels, the classifier would be able to distinguish between the two classes. So in this post, we will use two training files, one for each label. We have an equal number of YES (true) and NO (false) labels. The files are in CSV format.

We observe that LDA + SVM obtains a much lower accuracy as compared to TFIDF+SVM and also LSA+SVM. Overall TFIDF+SVM had the best performance. LDA considers each provision as a mixture of hidden topics and each topic as a mixture of words. The topics generated by LDA (in the articles and NIM provisions) were quite diferent when the articles and NIM provisions used diferent words, as it was not trained on a large corpus. Also articles and provisions are short texts and thus would not have much topic variation like documents. This infuenced the classification and resulted in a lower accuracy as compared to TFIDF + SVM.
